The Job

This is my first commissioned work. A friend of mine is friends with Nick - the singer of a band called The Hammers. Nick wanted a sledgehammer shaped microphone holder as a prop to use on stage.

My friend, Greg, and I worked on it together. I did the CAD work (in SolidWorks) and the 3D printing (on my Makergear M2), while Greg modified a typical microphone stand to work with the design.

Design

Here are some of the key design decisions that went into this project:

  • Using threaded inserts in the wooden handle to allow us to use bolts to attach the head.
  • Using two bolts instead of one. One bolt would require it to be very tight to prevent the head from spinning, which might crack the 3D printed plastic.
  • Printing it in two halves. This made for more manageable print times.
  • Using the design of the sledgehammer head to my advantage by putting screw holes in to attach the two halves together.
  • Using a removable microphone holder. This allows the head to accept many different shapes and sizes of microphone.
  • Having nothing be permanent and everything be easily replacable. I didn't know what abuse this thing would be withstanding, so avoiding glue and making parts modular helped avoid long redesigns or reprints.

Prototyping

I printed the microphone holder first, since it's relatively small and quick to print. After a few design iterations, I ended up with this:

I couldn't find any actual information about the taper of the microphone, but I made a few measurements with some calipers, and was able to make a pretty good fit that grips the microphone tightly. Additionally, the holder is thin enough to bend and conform slightly to the shape of the microphone.

Production

Each half of the microphone took over four hours to print. Being able to print the head in two halves was a huge help here. Here's the microphone in one half, waiting for the other half to be printed. You can see the part of the stand that holds the handle. It uses a pre-made guitar stand clamp, because the microphone is meant to be able to be carried around just like a sledgehammer.

Final Product

Overall, the man who commissioned the work, Nick, was very happy with the result. The day we finished, his band actually played at a party, and it survived all the roughhousing with no damage! Here are some pictures someone took.

Difficulties

Overall, the hammer turned out great and is very sturdy. However, there were a few hiccups:

  • The holes in the top that the screws pass through are very large and visually disruptive. It would be best to cap them somehow, but I wasn't able to think of a great solution in the amount of time I had.
  • Working the clearance for the removable microphone holder was a big pain. Even though I designed in what I thought was enough clearance, I had to do a lot of sanding and scraping to get it to slide into the dovetail slot in the head. I guess it turns out FDM isn't great for making parts that fit together nicely.
